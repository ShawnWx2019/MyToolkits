1 Introduction

In genome-wide association studies (GWAS) and quantitative genetics, the assumption of normality for trait residuals is critical for linear mixed models (LMMs) and standard regression analyses. Non-normally distributed phenotypes can lead to inflated Type I errors (false positives) or reduced statistical power.

This document details the standardization and transformation methods implemented in the transform_pheno function of the MyToolkits package, providing mathematical definitions, appropriate use cases, and high-level literature references.

2 Transformation Methods

2.1 Logarithmic Transformation (log2, log10)

Logarithmic transformations are the most common approach for handling heavily right-skewed quantitative traits, such as gene expression levels, metabolite abundances, or pathogen loads. It converts multiplicative relationships into additive ones, stabilizing the variance across the data range.

Mathematical Definition:

\[y_i = \log_b(x_i + c)\]

Where \(b\) is the base (typically 2 or 10), and \(c\) is an offset constant added to avoid \(\log(0)\) when the data contains zeros.

Use Case: Highly skewed continuous data where the variance increases with the mean.

Reference:

Visscher, P. M., Wray, N. R., et al. (2017). 10 Years of GWAS Discovery: Biology, Function, and Translation. The American Journal of Human Genetics, 101(1), 5-22. (Discusses general phenotype preparation and handling of skewed quantitative traits).

2.2 Z-score Standardization (zscore)

Z-score standardization centers the phenotype to a mean of 0 and scales it to a standard deviation of 1. While it does not change the underlying shape of the distribution (e.g., it will not make skewed data normal), it places different traits on a comparable scale.

Mathematical Definition:

\[z_i = \frac{x_i - \mu}{\sigma}\]

Use Case: When comparing effect sizes (Beta values) across multiple traits measured in different units (e.g., comparing plant height in cm with yield in kg/ha), or when calculating Polygenic Risk Scores (PRS).

Reference:

Bulik-Sullivan, B. K., et al. (2015). LD Score regression distinguishes confounding from polygenicity in genome-wide association studies. Nature Genetics, 47(3), 291-295.

2.3 Arcsine Square Root Transformation (arcsine_sqrt)

The arcsine square root transformation is historically used for proportional or percentage data (strictly bounded between 0 and 1) to stabilize variances, which tend to be smaller near the boundaries (0 and 1) and larger near the center (0.5).

Mathematical Definition:

\[y_i = \arcsin(\sqrt{x_i})\]

Use Case: Disease infection rates, tissue damage percentages, or DNA methylation \(\beta\)-values.

Reference:

Sokal, R. R., & Rohlf, F. J. (1995). Biometry: the principles and practice of statistics in biological research. W. H. Freeman and Co. (A foundational text validating variance stabilization for binomial proportions).

2.4 Logit Transformation (logit)

The logit transformation (log-odds) is an alternative and often preferred method for proportion data. Unlike the arcsine transformation, the logit function maps values from the interval \((0, 1)\) to the entire real line \((-\infty, \infty)\), making it highly suitable for linear modeling.

Mathematical Definition:

\[y_i = \ln\left(\frac{x_i}{1 - x_i}\right)\]

Use Case: Frequently used in epigenome-wide association studies (EWAS) to transform methylation \(\beta\)-values into M-values, providing better statistical performance in linear models.

Reference:

Du, P., Zhang, X., Huang, C. C., et al. (2010). Comparison of Beta-value and M-value methods for quantifying methylation levels by microarray analysis. BMC Bioinformatics, 11, 587. (Demonstrates the statistical superiority of logit/M-values over raw proportions).

2.5 Rank-Based Inverse Normal Transformation (int)

The Inverse Normal Transformation (INT) is the “gold standard” in modern GWAS for handling complex, non-normal quantitative traits. It is a non-parametric mapping that forces the sample distribution perfectly into a standard normal distribution. We utilize Blom’s formula, which is the most robust and widely accepted estimator in human and plant genetics.

Mathematical Definition:

\[y_i = \Phi^{-1}\left(\frac{r_i - c}{N - 2c + 1}\right)\]

Where \(\Phi^{-1}\) is the quantile function of the standard normal distribution, \(r_i\) is the ordinary rank of the \(i\)-th observation, \(N\) is the sample size, and Blom’s constant \(c = \frac{3}{8}\).

Use Case: GWAS of quantitative traits where the regression residuals severely violate the normality assumption, minimizing the impact of outliers and reducing false-positive associations.

References:

McCaw, Z. R., Lane, J. M., Saxena, R., Redline, S., & Lin, X. (2020). Operating characteristics of the rank-based inverse normal transformation for quantitative trait analysis in genome-wide association studies. Biometrics, 76(4), 1262-1272.

Beasley, T. M., Erickson, S., & Allison, D. B. (2009). Rank-based inverse normal transformations are increasingly used, but are they merited? Behavior Genetics, 39(5), 580-595.

3 Application in MyToolkits

The transform_pheno function is fully vectorized and dplyr compatible. Here is an example of how to seamlessly integrate it into a modern data analysis pipeline prior to GWAS:

Code 
library(dplyr)
library(MyToolkits)

# Assuming 'pheno_data' is your raw phenotype table
prepared_gwas_data <- pheno_data %>%
  mutate(
    # Standard normal transformation for a highly skewed yield trait
    Yield_INT = transform_pheno(Yield, method = "int"),
  
    # Logit transformation for disease incidence (bounded 0 to 1)
    Disease_Logit = transform_pheno(Disease_Rate, method = "logit"),
  
    # Standardizing environmental covariates
    Temp_Z = transform_pheno(Temperature, method = "zscore")
  )
